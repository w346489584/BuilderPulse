### What is today's most counter-intuitive finding?

**A Polymarket bot that always bets "No" on everything is profitable.**

[Nothing Ever Happens](https://github.com/sterlingcrispin/nothing-ever-happens) by @sterlingcrispin ([395 points, 214 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47753472)) is a bot that automatically buys "No" on every non-sports Polymarket prediction market. The thesis: prediction markets have a systematic bias toward dramatic outcomes because bettors are drawn to exciting "Yes" scenarios (will X happen?). The boring outcome — nothing happens — is consistently underpriced.

This is counter-intuitive because prediction markets are supposed to be efficient. If there were a systematic bias, sophisticated traders should arbitrage it away. But the bot's existence (and the 395-point HN discussion) suggests the bias is persistent enough to exploit programmatically.

The deeper insight for builders: **The default outcome in most markets is "nothing changes."** If you're building prediction features, recommendation engines, or trend detectors, you should calibrate against a "nothing happens" baseline. Most products over-index on novelty and change, when the most accurate prediction is usually stasis.

A parallel finding: in the "What Are You Working On?" thread, the projects that resonated most weren't AI moonshots — they were mundane solutions to boring problems. @cmos built a picture frame that monitors whether his elderly mother is still moving. @marcusdev built a train journey planner because UK rail search is bad. The "nothing exciting" approach wins.

**Takeaway**: Build the boring version. Bet on "No." The most profitable position in any market is usually the one that predicts nothing dramatic will happen — and the same principle applies to product development. The unglamorous, reliable, slightly-better-than-the-status-quo product usually wins.
