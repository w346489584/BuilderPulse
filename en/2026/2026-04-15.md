## Tech Radar

### Did any major company shut down or downgrade a product?

**Backblaze silently downgraded its backup coverage.** The company stopped backing up OneDrive folders, Dropbox folders, VeraCrypt encrypted drives, and .git directories without notifying users. From the [original article](https://rareese.com/posts/backblaze/): "Despite claiming to backup all your data, Backblaze quietly stopped backing up OneDrive and Dropbox folders." The "files on demand" feature is the technical trigger — @Neil44 explains: "You could sync a 1TB OneDrive to your 250GB laptop but it's OK because of selective sync. Then Backblaze tries to back the folder up and requests a download of every single file and now you have zero bytes free." But the community outrage is about the silence, not the technical constraint.

**Fiverr's security incident** is effectively a service-level downgrade. Customer files including tax returns, SSNs, and business documents are publicly indexed by Google. @gregsadetsky wrote to security@fiverr.com and received: "You're the second person to flag this issue to us... We are currently working to resolve the situation." The response time and severity suggest systemic security failures.

**OpenAI faces investor scrutiny.** [Reuters reports](https://www.reuters.com/legal/transactional/openai-investors-question-852-billion-valuation-strategy-shifts-ft-reports-2026-04-14/) that investors are questioning OpenAI's $852 billion valuation amid strategy shifts. Not a shutdown, but a significant confidence signal.

**Google announced a new spam policy** targeting ["back button hijacking"](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2026/04/back-button-hijacking) (836 points, 476 comments). Sites using history manipulation to trap users on exit will be penalized. This effectively kills a common dark pattern.

**Spain is expanding internet blocks** beyond football to [tennis, golf, and movie broadcasting times](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47768195) (402 points, 404 comments). This is a service-level downgrade for an entire country's internet infrastructure.

**Takeaway**: The Backblaze story is the week's most instructive failure. When your product's value proposition is "we back up everything," silently excluding categories is an existential brand crisis.

### What is today's most counter-intuitive finding?

**The WordPress supply chain attack uses Ethereum smart contracts for command-and-control — and it's more sophisticated than most AI-powered attacks.**

From the [original article](https://anchor.host/someone-bought-30-wordpress-plugins-and-planted-a-backdoor-in-all-of-them/): "It resolved its C2 domain through an Ethereum smart contract, querying public blockchain RPC endpoints. The attack details: A buyer using the name "Daley Tias" purchased 30+ WordPress plugins on Flippa for six figures. The backdoors lay dormant for 8 months. When activated, the `wpos-analytics` module phoned home to `analytics.essentialplugin.com`, downloaded a backdoor disguised as a WordPress core file (`wp-comments-posts.php` mimicking `wp-comments-post.php`), and injected PHP into wp-config.php. The injected code served SEO spam only to Googlebot — completely invisible to site owners.

@chromacity on Hacker News: "This is a perfect illustration of what cracks me up about the hyperbolic reactions to Mythos. We've built our existing tech stacks and corporate governance structures for a different era."

@edg5000: "If the plugins were bought for six figures, then it must be incredibly lucrative. How on earth could they be making it back? Is injecting spam into Google results THAT lucrative?"

**Why this is counter-intuitive**: While the tech world debates AI-powered vulnerability discovery (Claude Mythos at +600% in Google Trends), the actual most sophisticated attack this week used no AI at all. It combined low-tech social engineering (buy plugins on Flippa), patient timing (8-month dormancy), and novel infrastructure (Ethereum C2) in a way that no automated scanner would catch. The biggest security threats aren't technical — they're governance failures. No amount of code scanning catches "the previous owner sold the project."

**Takeaway**: The next WordPress-style supply chain attack target could be npm packages, VS Code extensions, or Claude Code skills. Any marketplace where trust transfers with ownership is vulnerable to this exact playbook.
